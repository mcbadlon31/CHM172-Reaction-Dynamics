Collision Theory

Reaction Dynamics • Physical Chemistry

Interactive Web Presentation

Core Concepts

Reactions occur when molecules collide if:

  1. They collide with sufficient frequency ($Z_{AB}$)
  2. They have enough kinetic energy ($\ge E_a$)
  3. They have correct orientation (Steric Factor $P$)

Goal:
Derive the Arrhenius Equation from first principles.

$$k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$$

Collision Density

The number of collisions per unit volume per unit time:

$$Z_{AB} = \sigma \left(\frac{8k_BT}{\pi \mu}\right)^{1/2} \mathcal{N}_A \mathcal{N}_B$$
$\sigma$: Collision Cross-Section (Target Area)
$\mu$: Reduced Mass ($\frac{m_A m_B}{m_A + m_B}$)

Molecular Speeds

Molecules have a distribution of speeds.

  • Mean speed: $\bar{v} = (8k_BT/\pi m)^{1/2}$
  • Relative speed: $\bar{v}_{rel} = (8k_BT/\pi \mu)^{1/2}$

Higher T $\to$ Broader distribution, higher mean speed.

Identical Molecules

For collisions between A and A ($Z_{AA}$):

$$Z_{AA} = \frac{1}{2} \sigma \left(\frac{8k_BT}{\pi \mu}\right)^{1/2} \mathcal{N}_A^2$$

The factor of $1/2$ prevents double-counting collisions.

Example: N$_2$ Collision Rate

Given: T = 298 K, P = 1 bar, $\sigma \approx 0.43$ nm$^2$

1. Number Density: $\mathcal{N} = P/k_BT \approx 2.43 \times 10^{25}$ m$^{-3}$

2. Mean Relative Speed: $\bar{v}_{rel} \approx 670$ m/s

3. Collision Density:

$$Z_{N_2-N_2} \approx 5 \times 10^{34} \text{ collisions m}^{-3} \text{ s}^{-1}$$
Insight: This rate is enormous! If every collision reacted, reactions would be instantaneous.

Energy Requirements

Not all collisions lead to reaction.

Only collisions with energy $\varepsilon \ge E_a$ react.

$$f(E > E_a) = e^{-E_a/RT}$$

This exponential factor dramatically reduces the effective rate.

Misconception Alert!

Myth: "Activation energy is the average energy of the molecules."

Reality:

  • $E_a$ is a threshold (minimum) energy.
  • Average energy ($\approx 1.5 RT$) is usually much lower than $E_a$.
  • Only the "lucky" few in the high-energy tail react.
Analogy: High Jump. The bar is $E_a$. Most athletes can't clear it; only the elite few succeed.

Fraction: Calculating...

Think-Pair-Share

Question: T increases from 300K to 310K (~3%). Rate doubles (~100%). Why?

  1. Collision frequency doubles.
  2. $E_a$ decreases.
  3. Fraction with $E > E_a$ increases exponentially.
  4. Molecules get bigger.

Answer: (C)

The exponential factor $e^{-E_a/RT}$ is extremely sensitive to temperature changes.

Derivation: The Setup

The total rate is the sum of rates over all possible energies:

$$Rate = \int_0^{\infty} \sigma(v_{rel}) v_{rel} \mathcal{N}_A \mathcal{N}_B f(v_{rel}) dv_{rel}$$

Key Substitutions:

  • $\sigma(E) = \sigma (1 - E_a/E)$ for $E \ge E_a$ (0 otherwise)
  • $v_{rel} = \sqrt{2E/\mu}$
  • $f(E)$ is the Boltzmann distribution

Derivation: The Integral

Substituting and integrating from $E_a$ to $\infty$:

$$k_r = \int_{E_a}^{\infty} \sigma \left(1 - \frac{E_a}{E}\right) \sqrt{\frac{2E}{\mu}} \frac{2\pi}{(\pi k_B T)^{3/2}} \sqrt{E} e^{-E/k_B T} dE$$

This looks scary, but it's a standard integral of the form $\int x e^{-x} dx$.

Derivation: The Result

The solution yields the Collision Theory Rate Constant:

$$k_r = N_A \sigma \left(\frac{8k_BT}{\pi \mu}\right)^{1/2} e^{-E_a/RT}$$

Success! This matches the Arrhenius form:

$$k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$$

The A-Factor

Collision theory predicts the pre-exponential factor:

$$A_{theory} = N_A \sigma \left(\frac{8k_BT}{\pi \mu}\right)^{1/2}$$
  • Typical value: $\sim 10^{11}$ dm$^3$ mol$^{-1}$ s$^{-1}$
  • Weak temperature dependence ($T^{1/2}$)

The Steric Factor ($P$)

Theory often overestimates the rate ($A_{theory} > A_{exp}$).

We introduce a correction factor $P$:

$$k = P \cdot A_{theory} e^{-E_a/RT}$$
$P < 1$: Strict orientation required (Steric Hindrance)
$P > 1$: Harpoon Mechanism (Long-range attraction)

The Harpoon Mechanism

Example: $K + Br_2 \to KBr + Br$ ($P \approx 4.8$)

  1. Electron jump from K to Br$_2$ at long range.
  2. Formation of $K^+$ and $Br_2^-$.
  3. Coulombic attraction pulls them together ("Harpoon").

Unimolecular Reactions

How can $A \to P$ be explained by collisions?

Lindemann-Hinshelwood Mechanism:

  1. Activation: $A + M \rightleftharpoons A^* + M$
  2. Reaction: $A^* \to P$

Lindemann Rate Law

$$k_{uni} = \frac{k_a k_b [M]}{k_a'[M] + k_b}$$
High Pressure:
First-order ($k_{uni} \approx k_\infty$)
Low Pressure:
Second-order ($k_{uni} \propto [M]$)
